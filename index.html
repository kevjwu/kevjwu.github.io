Online Learning Techniques for Portfolio Allocation

COMS 6998.001, “Bandits and Reinforcement Learning” Final Project (December 2017) | Full Text | Slides | Code

We undertake an empirical study of regret-minimizing online learning algorithms, in particular, the widely-cited Exponentiated Gradient (EG) algorithm proposed by Helmbold et al in 1998. We rerun parts of Helmbold's various on modern-day US stock market data and find that the algorithm fails to significantly outperform benchmark portfolios from 2000 to the present. We identify particular situations in which a multiplicative weight update algorithm like EG is more likely to fail, and also offer up possible explanations for its poor returns on post-2000 equity data.

Finally, we also present a novel application of EG in which wealth is allocated among trading strategies rather than directly to stocks; we find that by allocating wealth to mean reversion-based strategies, one can not only significantly outperform benchmark and EG portfolios, but also come close to achieving the returns optimal (regret-minimizing) portfolio allocation.

Understanding “Fedspeak”: Identifying the Sources of Market Sentiment in Central Bank Communications

Senior thesis, presented to the Harvard University Department of Applied Mathematics (April 2014) | Full Text

I examined the effect of one of the Federal Open Market Committee’s post-meeting announcement—one of the most highly anticipated economic release in the financial community—on short-term stock market returns. Whereas the relationship between Twitter or financial news and the polarity of Fed statements has been the subject of extensive research, I directly used returns on stock market indices to categorize statements as positive or negative. The other novelty of this approach was the use of L1-regression to identify a small number of influential phrases from central bank announcements from all possible n-grams.
